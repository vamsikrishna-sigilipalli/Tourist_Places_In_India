QUTUB MINAR

About Qutub Minar

Qutub Minar, located in Delhi, India, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the tallest brick minarets in the world, standing at a height of 73 meters (240 feet). It was commissioned by Qutb al-Din Aibak, the founder of the Delhi Sultanate, in 1193 to mark the beginning of Muslim rule in India. The construction of the minaret was completed by his successor, Iltutmish, and it features intricate carvings and inscriptions from the Quran, showcasing the exquisite craftsmanship of Indo-Islamic architecture. The Qutub Minar is made of red sandstone, adorned with beautiful calligraphy, and is surrounded by several historically significant structures, including the Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque, the first mosque in India. The architectural style of Qutub Minar reflects a blend of Persian and Indian influences, evident in its fluted design and ornamental detailing. The structure is not only an important historical monument but also serves as a symbol of the cultural and religious amalgamation that occurred during the era of the Delhi Sultanate. Visitors are drawn to its grandeur and historical significance, making it a prominent tourist attraction in India. Additionally, the site is often associated with various legends and stories, enhancing its mystique and allure.
